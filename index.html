Occupational Safety and Health Act

This page gives a simple classroom-style overview of worker safety rights and responsibilities. It is based on general jobsite safety requirements such as reporting injuries, using proper protective equipment, keeping work areas clean, and following warning signs.

What are 5 key rights?

1. The Right to Know

Workers have the right to know about hazards on the jobsite, including unsafe conditions, warning signs, restricted areas, and dangerous materials.

2. The Right to Protective Equipment

Employees should have the proper protective equipment before starting work. If they are unsure what is needed, they should ask a supervisor.

3. The Right to Report Injuries and Hazards

All injuries and unsafe conditions should be reported immediately. Workers should not stay silent when they see something dangerous.

4. The Right to Safe Tools and Equipment

Defective or worn equipment should be repaired or replaced. Workers should not be expected to use unsafe tools or machinery.

5. The Right to a Safe Work Area

Clear walkways, proper cleanup, safe storage, and obeying warning signs all help create a safer jobsite for everyone.

What is one myth people believe?

Myth: “Safety is only the supervisor’s job.”

This is false. Safety is everyone’s responsibility on a jobsite.

Workers must report injuries, report unsafe conditions, keep their work area clean, avoid horseplay, follow warning signs, and use tools properly.

A safe workplace depends on both supervisors and employees working together.

What could go wrong if employees don’t know this?

  • Injuries may not be reported in time — which can make treatment, records, and follow-up much harder.
  • Unsafe conditions may continue — such as defective tools, cluttered walkways, or poor housekeeping that can cause trips and falls.
  • Serious machinery accidents can happen — loose clothing, unsecured hair, or unauthorized use of controls can lead to major injury.
  • Fire or explosion risks can increase — especially when flammable materials are stored improperly or safety rules during refueling are ignored.
  • Workers may enter dangerous restricted areas — such as electrical, compressor, crane, or radiation hazard zones.

Examples of General Safety Requirements

  • Analyze the job from a safety standpoint before starting work.
  • Report all injuries and unsafe conditions to the supervisor immediately.
  • Do not engage in horseplay, mischief, or fighting on the jobsite.
  • Keep hair, clothing, and accessories away from machinery.
  • Keep work areas, floors, and walkways clean and free of hazards.
  • Do not use defective equipment; report it for repair or replacement.
  • Only authorized personnel should operate certain machines or enter restricted areas.
  • Obey all warning signs such as “Keep Out,” “No Smoking,” and “Eye Protection Required.”
