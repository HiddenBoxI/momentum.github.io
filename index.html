Occupational Safety and Health Act

Definition of Occupational Health and Safety

Definition

Occupational health and safety refers to the practices, policies, and procedures designed to protect workers from hazards, injuries, and illnesses in the workplace.

Its goal is to ensure a safe and healthy working environment by identifying risks, preventing accidents, and promoting safe behavior on the jobsite.

What are 5 key rights?

1. The Right to Know

Workers have the right to know about workplace hazards, warning signs, restricted areas, and unsafe conditions.

2. The Right to Protective Equipment

Workers should have the protective equipment needed before starting a job, including eye protection and other required safety gear.

3. The Right to Report Hazards

Employees have the right to report unsafe conditions immediately to their supervisor without ignoring or hiding the danger.

4. The Right to Report Injuries

All injuries must be reported right away so that the worker can receive help and the incident can be properly documented.

5. The Right to Safe Tools and Work Areas

Workers should not be expected to use defective equipment or work in areas filled with slipping, tripping, or other avoidable hazards.

Worker Responsibilities (Occupational Safety and Health Act)

According to the Occupational Safety and Health Act of 1970, employees are required to comply with all occupational safety and health standards, as well as all rules, regulations, and orders that apply to their work.

Each worker has a responsibility to follow safety procedures, use proper protective equipment, and report unsafe conditions or injuries to their supervisor immediately.

These rules are enforced by OSHA, which helps ensure that both employers and employees maintain a safe working environment.

What is one myth people believe?

Myth: “Safety is only the supervisor’s job.”

This is false. Safety is everyone’s responsibility on the jobsite.

Employees must follow warning signs, report injuries, report unsafe conditions, use tools properly, and keep their work areas clean.

What could go wrong if employees don’t know this?

  • Injuries may go unreported and workers may not get proper treatment or support in time.
  • Unsafe conditions may continue if no one reports defective tools, poor housekeeping, or restricted hazards.
  • Machinery accidents can happen if loose clothing, hair, or unsafe behavior is ignored.
  • Fire and chemical hazards can increase if flammable materials are handled improperly.
  • Workers may enter dangerous areas such as electrical, crane, compressor, or radiation zones without authorization.

General Safety Requirements for Every Jobsite

  • Analyze the job from a safety standpoint before starting work.
  • Report unsafe conditions to the supervisor immediately.
  • All injuries must be reported immediately.
  • Horseplay, mischief, and fighting are not tolerated on the jobsite.
  • Workers must not come to work under the influence of alcohol or unauthorized drugs.
  • Hair, loose clothing, and accessories must be kept away from machinery.
  • Each employee is responsible for keeping the work area clean and free of slipping or tripping hazards.
  • Defective or worn equipment should be reported for repair or replacement.
  • Only authorized personnel may operate certain controls, cranes, or restricted equipment.
  • Warning signs such as “Keep Out,” “No Smoking,” and “Eye Protection Required” must always be obeyed.
  • Fire extinguishers should be used only for minor fires, while major fires must be handled by the fire department.
