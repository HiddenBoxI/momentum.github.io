Workplace Safety & Reliability

This website explains two important workplace topics: occupational safety and reliability. It also shows how work ethic, quality, and reliability work together to make someone a valuable employee.

Occupational Safety and Health

Occupational health and safety refers to the practices, policies, and procedures used to protect workers from hazards, injuries, and unsafe conditions in the workplace.

The goal is to create a safe and healthy environment by identifying risks, preventing accidents, and encouraging safe behavior on every jobsite.

5 Key Rights

Right to Know Workers should know the hazards and risks on the jobsite.
Right to Protective Equipment Workers should have proper safety equipment before starting work.
Right to Report Hazards Unsafe conditions should be reported immediately.
Right to Report Injuries Workers should report injuries and receive help right away.
Right to a Safe Workplace Workers should not be expected to use unsafe tools or work in dangerous conditions.

Worker Responsibilities

Workers must follow safety rules, use proper equipment, report hazards and injuries, and help keep the work area safe for everyone. Safety is not only the supervisor’s job — it is everyone’s responsibility.

Reliability in the Workplace

Reliability means being dependable and trustworthy. A reliable worker follows through on commitments, meets deadlines, communicates when problems come up, and can be counted on over time.

Work Ethic vs Quality vs Reliability

Work Ethic

  • Showing up on time
  • Putting in consistent effort
  • Taking initiative
  • Staying focused
  • Being accountable

Example: A plumbing apprentice arrives on time every morning, gets tools ready before work starts, listens carefully to instructions, and stays busy instead of waiting to be told what to do.

Quality

  • Attention to detail
  • Accuracy and neatness
  • Meeting standards
  • Doing work properly
  • Producing reliable results

Example: A worker measures pipe carefully, makes clean cuts, checks fittings before installation, and completes the task neatly so the system works properly and does not leak.

Reliability

  • Following through on commitments
  • Meeting deadlines
  • Being dependable
  • Communicating issues
  • Maintaining consistency over time

Example: A worker promises to finish a task by the end of the day, keeps the supervisor updated if there is a delay, and consistently completes assigned work without needing constant reminders.

How They Work Together

Work ethic drives how hard you try, quality reflects how well you do the work, and reliability shows whether people can count on you every time. When all three are strong, you become a highly valued worker in any workplace.

Activity: Who Would You Hire?

Person A: Always on time, works hard, but makes frequent mistakes.

Person B: Does excellent work, but is often late and misses deadlines.

Question: Who would you hire and why?

  • Discuss with a partner or small group for 2–3 minutes.
  • Choose one person and explain your reason.
  • Share your answer with the class.

Suggested answer: I would choose Person A, because work ethic and reliability are harder to teach. Quality can usually improve with practice, training, and feedback. A dependable worker can grow, but an unreliable worker can negatively affect the whole team.
